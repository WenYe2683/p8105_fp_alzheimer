Tentative Title

Mapping and Modeling Alzheimer’s Mortality and Cognitive Decline in the U.S. (2015-2022)

Motivation

Alzheimer’s disease (AD) is one of the leading causes of death among older adults in the United States and a growing public health challenge as the population ages. According to the Alzheimer’s Association, approximately 7.2 million Americans aged 65 or above are projected to be living with Alzheimer’s in 2025, a number expected to rise significantly in coming decades. Understanding how Alzheimer’s mortality and self-reported cognitive decline vary across states - and how these patterns relate to social, environmental, and health factors - can inform prevention and policy efforts.

Our project aims to explore temporal and geographic trends in Alzheimer’s disease mortality and prevalence from 2015-2022 and examine how these are associated with contextual factors such as cardiovascular disease burden, education level, and air quality.

Intended Final Products

  • A reproducible written report summarising data sources, cleaning process, analyses, and findings

  • An interactive project website including summary dashboards and state-level visualizations

  • A 2-minute narrated screencast presenting key results and interpretations

  • A public GitHub repository with all code, data wrangling scripts, and documentation

Anticipated Data Sources

  1. CDC WONDER - Multiple Cause of Death Data (1999-2022)

    • Variables of interest:

      • Age-adjusted mortality rate per 100,000

      • State, year, and demographic breakdowns (age group, sex, race)

    • Usage in project: primary measure of Alzheimer’s mortality rates by state and year

    • URL: https://wonder.cdc.gov/mcd.html

  2. CDC Alzheimer’s Disease and Healthy Aging Data Portal (2015-2022)

    • Variables of interest:

      • Prevalence rate

      • Cognitive decline for Alzheimer’s-related outcomes

    • Usage in project: proxy measure for Alzheimer’s prevalence

    • URL: https://data.cdc.gov/Healthy-Aging/Alzheimer-s-Disease-and-Healthy-Aging-Data/hfr9-rurv/about_data

  3. CDC BRFSS Chronic Disease Indicators

    • Variables of interest: prevalence of covariates - cardiovascular disease, diabetes, hypertention, etc.

    • Usage: association measure between Alzheimer’s and cardiovascular comorbidities

    • URL: https://data.cdc.gov

  4. U.S. Census American Community Survey (ACS)

    • Variables of interest:

      • Percent of adults aged 25+ with a bachelor’s degree or higher (education)

      • Median household income (socioeconomic covariate)

    • Usage: covariates for education level and socioeconomic context

    • URL: https://data.census.gov

  5. EPA Air Quality System (AQS) Data

    • Variables of interest: annual average PM2.5 concentration and Ozone levels by state/year

    • Usage: environmental factors for modeling Alzheimer’s disease burden

    • URL: https://www.epa.gov/outdoor-air-quality-data

Planned Analyses / Visualizations / Coding Challenges

  1. Data Wrangling

    • Import and clean state-year-level data from multiple sources

    • Harmonize variable names (e.g., state, year, rate, prevalence) and join datasets

    • Handle missing or suppressed rates for low-population states

  2. Exploratory Analysis

    • Create time-series plots showing trends in Alzheimer’s mortality

    • Generate choropleth heatmaps comparing state-level mortality and cognitive decline

    • Summarize national trends and identify outlier states

  3. Modeling & Association

    • Use multiple linear regression or mixed-effects models to examine associations between Alzheimer’s outcomes and risk factors such as cardiovascular prevalence, education, and air quality

    • Assess potential interactions (e.g., region × year)

  4. Visualization & Communication

    • Develop a dashboard-style web page

    • Use packages such as ggplot2, plotly, sf and tidycensus for visualizations

    • Emphasize clear labeling, color-blind-friendly palettes, and interpretability

  5. Coding Challenges

    • Merging datasets from multiple public APIs with different formats

    • Managing large CDC WONDER exports and maintaining reproducibility with code-based data pulls

    • Ensuring consistent geospatial joins and age-adjusted comparisons
