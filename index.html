Cognitive Decline & Alzheimer’s Disease

Motivation and Project Overview

As U.S. Alzheimer’s disease (AD) cases are projected to reach 7.2 million by 2025, identifying environmental and social risk factors such as age, sex and income, is urgent. We aim to inform prevention efforts by analyzing state-level variations in AD data. Our project investigates temporal and geographic trends in AD mortality and prevalence (2015-2016) to determine their association with education levels and air quality.

Importance and Desire of Longevity

Over the years, people wonder about the maximum lifespan for human beings and how to achieve longevity with cognitive health. To that end, scientists have published numerous articles exploring perspectives such as genetics, diet, exercise, lifestyle habits, attitude, and stress management to prolong human life and maintain cognitive function. Although life expectancy has reached an 80-year high, limited resources and environmental challenges have made the goal of maximizing human cognitive health challenging. Understanding the factors that contribute to cognitive decline is crucial for developing effective prevention strategies.

Associated Factors

Cognitive Decline and Age
Age is the strongest known risk factor for cognitive decline and Alzheimer's disease. The prevalence of cognitive problems increases significantly with age, particularly after 65 years. Understanding age-related patterns helps identify critical intervention points.
Cognitive Decline and Socioeconomic Status
Higher socioeconomic status, including education and income levels, is associated with lower rates of cognitive decline. Education may provide cognitive reserve, while income affects access to healthcare, healthy lifestyles, and living conditions that support brain health.
Cognitive Decline and Environmental Factors
Environmental exposures, particularly air pollution (PM2.5), have been linked to increased risk of cognitive decline and neurodegenerative diseases. Long-term exposure to poor air quality may accelerate cognitive aging and increase dementia risk.

Research Questions

Our analysis addresses the following key questions:

  1. Geographic Variation: How does the prevalence of self-reported cognitive decline vary across U.S. states and territories?
  2. Demographic Patterns: Are there differences in cognitive burden by age group and sex?
  3. Socioeconomic Factors: Is state-level median household income associated with cognitive decline prevalence?
  4. Educational Attainment: Does the proportion of adults with higher education relate to cognitive outcomes?
  5. Environmental Exposures: Is air pollution (PM2.5) associated with cognitive decline at the state level?
